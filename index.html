Reaction Coordinates: Effects of a Catalyst and Added Heat (ΔT = +30 °C)

Visualizing energy changes along a reaction coordinate, then applying the idea to a cannabis-cultivation reaction.

1) General Reaction Coordinate

  • Reactants → Products along the x-axis (progress of reaction).
  • Energy barrier (Eₐ) must be overcome to form products.
  • ΔE indicates overall energy change (endo/exothermic depending on sign).

2) Effect of a Catalyst (dotted line “a”)

Rate effect: Lower Eₐ ⇒ larger fraction of collisions cross the barrier ⇒ faster reaction. Thermodynamics (ΔE) is unchanged; only the pathway and kinetics change.

100-word explanation (editable)

Introducing a catalyst creates an alternative pathway with a lower activation energy (Eₐ). Because fewer joules are required for the transition-state to form, a larger fraction of molecular collisions have sufficient energy at the same temperature. This increases the rate constant (k) without changing reactant or product energies, so ΔE and the reaction’s thermodynamic favorability remain unchanged. In other words, catalysts accelerate how quickly equilibrium is reached but do not alter the equilibrium position. On a reaction coordinate diagram, this appears as a dotted curve with a lower peak; arrows to the barrier emphasize the kinetic advantage gained.

3) Effect of Added Heat (+30 °C) (dotted line “b”)

Rate effect: Raising temperature doesn’t change Eₐ or ΔE on the diagram, but it increases the fraction of molecules with energy ≥ Eₐ (Arrhenius). Result: higher k and a faster rate. Your dotted line “b” is included per instructions, with a note that the barrier is conceptually unchanged.

100-word explanation (editable)

Increasing temperature by ~30 °C raises average molecular kinetic energy and broadens the Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution. Even though the activation energy (Eₐ) is the same, a larger fraction of molecules now have energies at or above Eₐ, so the rate constant k increases (k = A·e^(−Eₐ/RT)). The energy landscape (reactants, transition state, products) is essentially unchanged, but the dynamics shift: more successful collisions per unit time, faster approach to equilibrium, and shorter half-life for the same reaction. This is why heating can mimic some rate benefits of catalysis, but unlike a catalyst, temperature raises energy for everything and can accelerate side reactions, too.

4) Applying Reaction Coordinates to Cannabis Cultivation: Nitrification in Grow Media

Why this matters in cultivation

  • Nitrification: NH₄⁺ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ by nitrifying microbes (e.g., Nitrosomonas, Nitrobacter); enzyme-catalyzed ⇒ lower Eₐ, faster conversion.
  • Temperature: Rates typically increase toward ~25–30 °C in media; colder rooms slow conversion (fewer molecules ≥ Eₐ).
  • pH coupling: Process releases H⁺, tending to lower pH over time—relevant for nutrient availability in cannabis.
100-word application (editable)

In cannabis cultivation, nitrification converts ammonium (NH₄⁺) to nitrate (NO₃⁻), the form roots readily uptake. The biology supplies the “catalyst”: microbial enzymes that lower the activation energy, dramatically accelerating the transformation compared with the uncatalyzed path. Temperature also modulates kinetics—near 25–30 °C, more molecular collisions exceed Eₐ, so conversion is faster; in cool media, rates drop. On a reaction coordinate, the enzyme-catalyzed curve shows a smaller barrier, while a temperature increase leaves Eₐ unchanged but increases the fraction of molecules above it. Practically, stable warm temperatures and healthy microbial populations support reliable nitrate availability and steadier feed schedules.
